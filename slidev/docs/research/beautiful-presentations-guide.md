## Section 1: Design Philosophy

### 1.1 Garr Reynolds — Presentation Zen

**The Three Pillars:**
- **Restraint** in preparation
- **Simplicity** in design
- **Naturalness** in delivery

**Core Doctrine: Reject Slideument Culture**

> "Think not in terms of decoration but in terms of clarity, a kind of clarity achieved through omission or exclusion of the non-essential." — Garr Reynolds

A "slideument" is a hybrid document-slide that tries to serve as both speaker notes and a standalone document. It fails at both. Slides support narration; they do not replace the speaker.

**Cognitive Science Foundation (Richard Mayer's research):**
- **Dual-channel principle:** People process visual and verbal information through separate channels. Don't double-load one channel.
- **Limited capacity:** Audiences absorb only a few pieces of information at once. More is not better.
- **Active processing:** Viewers learn by actively organizing material — not passively receiving bullet-point dumps.

**The Segmenting Principle:** Break complex content across multiple slides rather than cramming onto one. People comprehend better with small information chunks.

**Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR):**
Every element either adds signal or adds noise. Remove: unnecessary grid lines, gratuitous logos on every slide, decorative borders, clip art, redundant labels. Keep only what directly supports the message.

> "Making the simple complicated is commonplace; making the complicated simple, awesomely simple, that's creativity." — Charles Mingus (cited by Reynolds)

> "The more strikingly visual your presentation is, the more people will remember it. And more importantly, they will remember you." — Paul Arden

**Key Rules:**
- Best slides may contain no text whatsoever
- Narration + pictures beats narration alone (proven by research)
- Never use narration + text + graphics simultaneously — the redundancy overloads cognitive systems
- Design for the back of the room: if the last row can't read it, redesign

---

### 1.2 Nancy Duarte — Resonate & Slide:ology

**The Sparkline Framework:**

Duarte's core insight: every great presentation oscillates between **"what is"** (current reality) and **"what could be"** (the compelling future). This contrast creates engagement. A flat line of "this is the situation" bores audiences. The rhythm of tension and release holds attention.

**STAR Moments (Significant Turn-Around Result):**

STAR moments are carefully engineered turning points where the audience achieves "shared meaning" with the presenter. They must be:
- **Simple** — break complexity into human-centered, transparent form
- **Transferable** — emotional impact moves from speaker to audience
- **Audience-centered** — requires deep audience analysis
- **Repeatable** — easily describable and worth sharing second-hand
- **Meaningful** — audience is motivated to spread the idea

**Types of STAR Moments:**
- Memorable dramatizations (e.g., Bill Gates releasing mosquitos on stage)
- Emotive storytelling with personal narrative
- Evocative visuals with emotional resonance
- Repeatable sound bites and catchphrases
- Shocking statistics presented compellingly

**"Lipstick on a pig" principle:** Having great slides with poor content achieves nothing. Content and delivery matter more than visual design.

> "Presentations are a 'glance media'... Ask whether your message can be processed effectively within three seconds." — Nancy Duarte

**Design Decisions Slide (pre-design framework):**

Before designing any slide, create a hidden "design decisions" slide establishing:
1. **Core colors** (2–3 maximum)
2. **Typefaces** (1–2 maximum, use weight variation instead of more fonts)
3. **Imagery approach** (photographs, icons, or combination)

**Practice Design, Not Decoration:**

> "Help them see what you are saying." — Nancy Duarte (Slide:ology)

Colors should resonate with your audience, your industry, and your own aesthetic. Use a palette of few colors consistently rather than choosing arbitrarily per slide.

### 1.3 Edward Tufte — Data-Ink & Visual Integrity

**The Five Core Principles:**
1. "Above all else show data"
2. "Maximize the data-ink ratio"
3. "Erase non-data-ink"
4. "Erase redundant data-ink"
5. "Revise and edit"

**Data-Ink Ratio:**

Data-ink = the non-erasable ink used to represent data. If data-ink is removed, the chart loses information content. Non-data-ink includes decorative borders, background fills, excessive grid lines, shadows, 3D effects.

Formula: `Data-Ink Ratio = Data-Ink / Total Ink Used`

Goal: maximize toward 1.0 (all ink serves data).

**Chartjunk:** All visual elements that distract from data without adding information. This includes:
- Unnecessary grid lines and tick marks
- 3D effects that distort values
- Moiré patterns and cross-hatching
- Decorative backgrounds behind charts
- Shadows and bevels on bars or pie slices

**Lie Factor:** When graphical representation distorts actual data differences. Example: a 53% numerical change represented by a 783% graphical change = Lie Factor of 14.8. Always represent data proportionally.

**Graphical Integrity Rules:**
- Never distort what the data actually says
- Clear, detailed, thorough labels eliminate the need for legends
- Show data variation, not design variation

**Named Techniques:**
- **Sparklines:** "Small, intense, simple, word-sized graphics" that appear inline with text — no axes, no decoration, pure trend signal
- **Small multiples:** Many versions of the same chart type at small scale for comparison
- **Range-frame scatterplot:** Axis lines only at the actual range of the data (not zero-to-max)

---

### 1.4 The Three Cardinal Sins

From synthesizing all three masters:

| Sin | What It Looks Like | Why It Fails |
|-----|-------------------|-------------|
| Slideuments | Dense paragraphs, 8-bullet slides | Audience reads ahead, stops listening |
| Chartjunk | 3D pies, gradient bars, shadow effects | Distorts data, adds cognitive load |
| Data dumping | Everything on one slide "for completeness" | Exceeds working memory, loses the message |

## Section 2: Typography Rules

### 2.1 Font Size Hierarchy

**Live Presentations (audience in room):**

| Element | Minimum | Target |
|---------|---------|--------|
| Title / Main headline | 40pt | 60pt+ |
| Section heading | 28pt | 40pt |
| Body text | 24pt | 32–40pt |
| Captions, labels, footers | 18pt | 20pt+ |

**Leave-behind / Read-ahead decks (PDF, email):**

| Element | Minimum | Target |
|---------|---------|--------|
| Title | 28pt | 40pt |
| Body text | 16pt | 20pt |
| Captions / footnotes | 10pt | 12pt |

**Sales / Training presentations (mixed use):**

| Element | Minimum | Target |
|---------|---------|--------|
| Titles | 20pt | 28pt+ |
| Headers | 16pt | 20pt+ |
| Body copy | 12pt | 16pt+ |
| Callout labels | 12pt | 18pt+ |

**The 30pt Rule (Guy Kawasaki):** Minimum 30pt for any text in presentations. The rationale: anything smaller lets you cram too much, tempting you to read slides verbatim. Kawasaki's algorithm: "find the age of the oldest person in your audience and divide by two — that's your optimal font size."

**Hierarchy constraint:** Use no more than 3 different font sizes per slide. Consistency is comprehension.

---

### 2.2 Font Selection

**Sans-serif dominates projected presentations:**
- Best for screen legibility at large sizes
- Recommended: **Inter, Montserrat, Lato, Open Sans, Roboto, DM Sans, Nunito, Poppins**
- All available free via Google Fonts

**Serif works for:**
- Formal documents and leave-behinds
- Large display text (headlines only)
- Recommended: **Playfair Display, Merriweather, Georgia, Garamond** (only at 36pt+)

**Slab serif (hybrid option):**
- Rockwell, Roboto Slab — excellent legibility on slides, distinctive character
- Works at headline level; avoid for body text

**Fonts to avoid on slides:**
- Comic Sans — universally unprofessional
- Papyrus — overused, low credibility
- Times New Roman (default) — signals no design effort
- Arial (default) — signals no design effort
- Any script/handwriting font for body text

---

### 2.3 Font Pairing Rules

**Rule 1: Maximum 2 fonts per presentation.** If using one font, exploit multiple weights (Light, Regular, Bold, Black).

**Rule 2: Contrast creates pairing.** Good pairs have high contrast:
- Serif heading + Sans-serif body (classic, timeless)
- Bold/display heading + Light/regular body (weight contrast)
- Geometric + Humanist (form contrast)

**Rule 3: Avoid pairing two fonts of the same category** unless you have a strong stylistic reason.

**Proven pairings for presentations:**

| Heading | Body | Character |
|---------|------|-----------|
| Playfair Display | Lato | Elegant, editorial |
| Montserrat Bold | Open Sans | Modern, corporate |
| Raleway | Nunito | Friendly, contemporary |
| DM Serif Display | DM Sans | Cohesive design system |
| Inter (Bold) | Inter (Regular) | Minimalist, tech |
| Oswald | Source Sans Pro | Strong, accessible |

**Ratio rule:** If your heading is 48pt, body should be roughly half: 24pt. The halving principle creates clear, legible hierarchy.

---

### 2.4 Typography Hierarchy Principles

**Three levels minimum, five maximum:**

1. **Display / Title** — One clear dominant statement
2. **Heading** — Section or slide subtitle
3. **Body** — Supporting content
4. *(Optional)* **Caption / Label** — Charts, footnotes, sources
5. *(Optional)* **Emphasis** — Bold callout within body

**Emphasis tools in order of impact:**
1. Size increase
2. Weight increase (Bold)
3. Color change
4. All-caps (use sparingly — DESTROYS readability at body size)
5. Italic (least effective for emphasis, better for citations)

**Never underline** — on screens, underline means hyperlink.

**Line spacing:** 1.2–1.5× for body text. Tighter for headlines (0.9–1.1×).

**Character spacing:** For large display text (60pt+), tighten tracking slightly (-20 to -40). For small body text, loosen slightly.

## Section 3: Color Theory

### 3.1 The 60-30-10 Rule

The fundamental palette distribution:
- **60%** — Dominant/background color (typically neutral: white, light grey, dark navy, charcoal)
- **30%** — Secondary color (supports and complements dominant)
- **10%** — Accent color (used for highlights, CTAs, key data points only)

Palette size: **2–3 primary colors + 1–2 accent colors maximum.**

Using more creates visual chaos and dilutes hierarchy.

---

### 3.2 Color Psychology for Presentations

| Color | Associations | Best Use Case | Caution |
|-------|-------------|---------------|---------|
| **Blue** | Trust, stability, credibility, calm | Finance, healthcare, corporate, tech | Can feel cold or distant |
| **Green** | Growth, health, balance, progress | Sustainability, finance, wellness | Can feel generic |
| **Red** | Urgency, passion, danger, energy | CTAs, warnings, emphasis, retail | Overuse = anxiety |
| **Orange** | Friendliness, creativity, warmth | Startups, consumer brands | Less professional |
| **Yellow** | Energy, optimism, attention | Highlights, education | Eye fatigue at high saturation |
| **Purple** | Luxury, wisdom, mystery | Premium, beauty, royalty | Narrow appeal |
| **Black** | Authority, elegance, power | Premium, tech, minimalist | Heavy; use with white space |
| **White** | Clarity, space, cleanliness | Backgrounds, breathing room | Needs strong accent colors |

**Warm colors (red, orange, yellow)** visually advance — use for foreground elements.
**Cool colors (blue, green, purple)** visually recede — use for backgrounds.

**Dark rooms:** Dark background + light text (maintains visual intensity, reduces eye strain).
**Lit rooms:** Light/white background + dark text (better legibility in ambient light).

---

### 3.3 Contrast & Accessibility

**WCAG AA standards (minimum for professional presentations):**
- Normal text (under 18pt regular / 14pt bold): **4.5:1 contrast ratio minimum**
- Large text (18pt+ regular or 14pt+ bold): **3:1 contrast ratio minimum**
- Graphical objects and UI components: **3:1 minimum**

**WCAG AAA standards (enhanced accessibility):**
- Normal text: **7:1 contrast ratio**
- Large text: **4.5:1 contrast ratio**

**Critical precision:** #777777 grey achieves a 4.47:1 ratio on white — this FAILS the 4.5:1 requirement. Never round up.

**Color blindness considerations:**
- ~8% of men have red-green color blindness
- Never use color as the **only** means of conveying information
- Supplement color with labels, patterns, or icons
- Avoid red-green and yellow-blue combinations for data encoding
- Test designs in greyscale: if the chart loses meaning, redesign

**Tool recommendations:** WebAIM Contrast Checker, Coolors, Color Hunt, Accessible Palette.

---

### 3.4 Color Do's and Don'ts

**Do:**
- Establish palette before opening slide software
- Use color to encode meaning consistently (green = positive, red = negative)
- Test on projector before final — projectors wash out colors, especially yellows
- Keep brand colors sacred in corporate contexts
- Use Garr Reynolds' rule: cool colors for backgrounds, warm colors for foreground text

**Don't:**
- Use more than 4 colors in a slide deck
- Apply rainbow palettes to charts (no meaning = just decoration)
- Use pure saturated colors; use earthier/muted versions (olive vs. neon green)
- Choose colors that look good on your monitor but fail on projection screens
- Rely on red-green differentiation for colorblind audiences

## Section 4: Layout & Composition

### 4.1 The Rule of Thirds

Divide the slide into a 3×3 grid with two horizontal and two vertical lines. This creates 9 equal rectangles and 4 natural focal points (intersections).

**Core application:**
- Place the most important content (headline, key visual, CTA) **at grid intersections**, not the center
- Position primary images along vertical or horizontal grid lines
- Reserve center for breathing room, not content density
- Use off-center placement for dynamic tension; centered only for title slides or emphasis quotes

**What the Rule of Thirds achieves:**
1. Asymmetrical balance (more visually interesting than centered layouts)
2. Natural eye flow (viewers scan from top-left, follow the grid)
3. Visual tension (elements off-center feel alive)

**Limit:** Focus on 1–2 key focal points per slide. Overloading gridlines defeats the purpose.

---

### 4.2 Whitespace (Negative Space)

Whitespace is not wasted space — it is an active design element. It:
- Isolates important content and makes it breathe
- Reduces cognitive load
- Creates perceived elegance and professionalism
- Guides the eye to focal points

**Rules:**
- Resist the urge to fill every inch of a slide
- More whitespace around an element = more visual weight for that element
- Consistent margins across all slides (minimum 5–8% of slide width on each side)
- Grouping: elements close together appear related; elements with space between them appear separate (Gestalt proximity principle)

**The professional telltale:** Amateur slides are densely packed. Professional slides have generous whitespace.

---

### 4.3 Grid Systems for Professional Consistency

**The 12-column grid** is the industry standard adapted from print and web design:

- **Two-column layout:** 6+6 (equal split for text/image pairs)
- **Asymmetric layout:** 4+8 or 8+4 (dominant content + supporting)
- **Three-column layout:** 4+4+4 (comparison slides, three-point frameworks)

**Key grid rules:**
- All content aligns to grid columns — no floating elements
- Consistent gutters (space between columns) across all slides: typically 2–3% of slide width
- Margins should be uniform across the entire deck
- Set up grid on master slide before designing any content slide

**Alignment is non-negotiable.** Misaligned elements read as careless, regardless of content quality.

---

### 4.4 Visual Hierarchy on the Slide

Elements compete for attention. Your job is to orchestrate a clear pecking order using:

**Size:** Larger = more important. The dominant element should be noticeably larger, not marginally.

**Color/Contrast:** High contrast = high attention. Use accent color for one key element per slide.

**Position:** Upper-left corner is read first in Western cultures. Place the most important element there, or at a rule-of-thirds intersection.

**Spacing:** Elements with more space around them have more visual weight.

**Weight:** Bold text has more visual mass than regular weight.

**Practical rule:** Squint at your slide. The most important element should still be obvious with blurred vision.

---

### 4.5 Slide Dimensions & Resolution

**Aspect ratio:**
- **16:9 widescreen** — use this for all modern presentations. It matches projectors, monitors, and screens.
- **4:3 standard** — only use for legacy projectors when explicitly required. Signals dated design.

**Resolution:**
- Minimum: 1920×1080 (Full HD) for exported images and fullscreen photos
- Images: PNG for graphics/screenshots (lossless), JPEG for photographs
- Never use images below 1920×1080 for full-bleed backgrounds
- Never stretch images — maintain aspect ratios

---

### 4.6 Slide Count and Timing

| Format | Slides | Duration | Source |
|--------|--------|----------|--------|
| Investor pitch (standard) | 10 | 20 min | Guy Kawasaki |
| Demo Day (YC) | 5–7 | 2 min | Y Combinator |
| TED Talk | No "right" number | 18 min max | TED |
| McKinsey client deck | 15–30 | 60 min | McKinsey |
| Sales / training deck | 20–40 | Variable | Best practice |

**Kawasaki's rule:** A normal human being cannot comprehend more than 10 concepts in a meeting. If you need more than 10 slides to explain your business, you likely don't have a viable business.

**McKinsey's rule:** Aim for 1 slide per minute of presentation. This forces prioritization.

## Section 5: Visual Storytelling

### 5.1 Narrative Structures

**Structure 1: The Hero's Journey (Monomyth)**

Best for: product launches, case studies, company origin stories, fundraising pitches.

Three-act structure adapted for presentations:

| Act | Purpose | Presentation Equivalent |
|-----|---------|------------------------|
| **Departure** | Status quo → call to adventure | Current situation → the problem |
| **Initiation** | Facing the villain → proving efficacy | Solution → benefits + evidence |
| **Return** | Transformed world | Future state → call to action |

**Pattern:** Near failure → hard-won triumph → return to improved normality.

**Structure 2: Duarte's Sparkline**

The oscillating pattern between "what is" and "what could be":

```
WHAT IS    ↓   ↑   ↓   ↑   ↓
           [tension] [release] [tension]
WHAT COULD BE         ↑         ↑
```

Every major beat either establishes current reality (grounding) or reveals the compelling possibility (inspiring). Alternate between these throughout the presentation.

**Structure 3: McKinsey SCR (Situation-Complication-Resolution)**

Best for: executive presentations, consulting reports, business cases.

- **Situation:** Current state that everyone accepts as true
- **Complication:** Disrupting factor that creates a problem or opportunity
- **Resolution:** Recommendation that directly addresses the complication

**Structure 4: The Pyramid Principle (Barbara Minto / McKinsey)**

Lead with the answer, then justify:
1. State your recommendation in the first slide
2. Present 3 key supporting points (human working memory limit)
3. Provide evidence under each supporting point

> "You think from the bottom up, but you present from the top down." — Barbara Minto

---

### 5.2 Emotional Design Principles

**Balance fact and emotion.** Duarte: imbalance damages credibility. Too much emotion = inspiring but untrustworthy. Too much data = accurate but forgettable.

**The audience is the hero, not you.** Your role is guide (Yoda), not protagonist (Luke). The audience's transformation is the story.

**Make it personal.** Concrete personal stories are remembered 22× more than statistics alone. Use the story to *prove* the statistic, not replace it.

**End with a clear call to action.** Every presentation asks the audience to do, believe, or feel something differently. State it explicitly.

---

### 5.3 Presentation as Glance Media

Nancy Duarte's principle: slides function like highway billboards, not textbooks. The audience drives past at 60mph. Your slide has 3 seconds to communicate.

**Test:** Can each slide be understood in 3 seconds? If not, simplify.

**Applications:**
- One idea per slide — always
- Title = the takeaway (not "Q3 Revenue" but "Revenue grew 14% driven by APAC")
- Visual first, text second
- The spoken word carries detail; the slide carries the headline

## Section 6: Data Visualization

### 6.1 Chart Selection Matrix

Start with the insight. Choose the chart that best reveals that insight.

| Goal | Best Chart | Avoid |
|------|-----------|-------|
| Compare categories | Horizontal bar chart | 3D bars, exploded pies |
| Show change over time | Line chart | Bar chart with many periods |
| Parts of a whole | Stacked bar / Treemap | Pie chart with 6+ slices |
| Correlation | Scatter plot + trendline | Tables |
| Distribution | Histogram / Box plot | Line chart |
| Process / flow | Sankey diagram / Flowchart | Bullet lists |
| Simple proportion | Donut chart (3–5 slices) | 3D pie |

---

### 6.2 Bar & Column Charts

**Inviolable rule:** The numerical axis must start at zero. Bar length encodes value. A truncated axis lies.

**Other rules:**
- Sort bars intentionally — highest to lowest, unless natural order (chronological, alphabetical) exists
- Use horizontal bars for long category names or many categories
- Apply a single color; use one accent color for the highlighted value
- Include data labels when they help; remove when they clutter
- Never use grouped bars beyond 3 series without simplifying first
- Never use 3D effects

---

### 6.3 Line Charts

- Line charts do NOT require zero baselines (they encode position + angle, not length)
- **Maximum 5–6 lines.** Beyond 6 = visual overload
- Label lines directly; eliminate legends when possible
- Use distinct colors only to differentiate series
- Use markers sparingly — too many markers = clutter
- Annotate important inflection points directly on the chart
- Keep gridlines light (20–30% opacity); never heavy

---

### 6.4 Pie & Donut Charts

- **Maximum 5 slices** (6 absolute maximum)
- Slices must total exactly 100%
- Arrange largest slice at 12 o'clock, clockwise descending
- Label slices directly — never rely on legend + matching colors
- Prefer **donut charts**: the empty center improves readability and allows a key number
- Never use 3D, exploded slices, or perspective effects
- If you need more than 6 categories, use a bar chart instead

---

### 6.5 Tables

- Right-align numbers; left-align text
- Consistent decimal places and formatting (commas, currency symbols)
- Use subtle shading (alternating rows or column headers) to aid scanning
- Highlight key cells with accent color
- Keep column names short and meaningful
- Do not use tables to show trends — use line charts

---

### 6.6 The Three Chart Design Principles (Garr Reynolds)

**Restrain:** Limit information. Edit ruthlessly. The chart should show one insight.

**Reduce:** Remove all non-essential elements. Ask "what is truly important?" for each element.

**Emphasize:** Use contrast — especially color — to highlight the key data point. Replace generic titles ("Sales by Region") with declarative statements ("APAC drove 68% of growth").

---

### 6.7 Universal Visualization Rules

**Start with the insight, choose the chart after:**
> "Use the simplest chart that works. Bar charts beat 3D pie charts every time." — McKinsey

**Eliminate:**
- Grid lines (or make them 10–20% opacity)
- 3D effects
- Shadows and bevels
- Decorative backgrounds behind charts
- Legends (label directly when possible)

**Always include:**
- Source citation (small text, lower left or bottom of chart)
- Chart title as declarative statement
- Units on axes

**Do the math for your audience:** Show the percentage change, not just raw numbers. "Revenue grew 23%" is clearer than "Revenue: $4.2M → $5.2M."

**Color in charts:**
- Monochromatic is often sufficient — color isn't always necessary
- Use one accent color for the key data series
- Avoid red-green differentiation (colorblind audiences)
- Test in greyscale — if the chart loses meaning, it was encoded by color alone

## Section 7: Modern Trends 2025–2026

### 7.1 Dominant Aesthetic Trends

**1. Bold Minimalism with High Contrast**
- White text on navy, yellow on charcoal, black on cream
- Large fonts (60pt+) as primary design element
- Maximum 1–2 elements per slide
- "Less is more" taken to its logical conclusion

**2. Editorial / Magazine Layout**
- Wide margins, asymmetric grids, strong photo hierarchy
- Pull quotes in large, contrasting type
- Clear reading flow with visual breathing room
- Inspired by editorial design (New Yorker, Monocle, The Economist)

**3. Dark Mode with Neon Accents**
- Black/navy/deep grey backgrounds
- Neon green, electric blue, or bold orange as accent
- Best for: tech pitches, product launches, premium brands
- High drama, strong contrast

**4. Human-Centric / Imperfect Design**
- Hand-drawn graphics, organic shapes
- Earthy color palettes (terracotta, sage, cream)
- Tactile, warm imagery
- Deliberate imperfection signals authenticity and trust

**5. Broken Grid / Asymmetric Layouts**
- Overlapping text and images
- Angled sections and diagonal dividers
- Feels dynamic, editorial, premium
- Requires confident execution — easily becomes chaos

**6. Retrofuturism**
- Vintage fonts (chunky serifs, retro sans) + modern color
- 1960s–1980s aesthetic references
- Creates timelessness and emotional nostalgia
- Bridges past and future for innovative messaging

**7. Vivid Gradients**
- Magenta to orange, cobalt to lime, violet to pink
- Sweeping full-bleed backgrounds
- Adds energy and modernity
- Avoid for data-heavy slides — conflicts with chart colors

**8. AI-Generated Visuals**
- Custom imagery replacing generic stock photos
- Tools: Midjourney, DALL-E, Ideogram, Canva AI
- Advantage: unique, brand-specific imagery
- Risk: inconsistent style without a clear art direction brief

**9. Glassmorphism**
- Frosted-glass effect: blurred background with soft transparent panels
- Creates depth and modern visual hierarchy
- Use for call-out boxes or content containers over photography

**10. Micro-Animations**
- Subtle element animations: bounce on entry, fade reveal
- Animated charts (bars rise, donuts fill)
- 0.3–0.5 second duration — shorter than 2016-era animations
- Timing: 0.5–1 second maximum per animation

---

### 7.2 What's Out in 2025–2026

- Default PowerPoint/Keynote templates (immediately readable as no-design-effort)
- Clip art and generic stock photography (handshakes, lightbulbs, targets)
- WordArt and text effects
- Excessive slide transitions (spinning, bouncing, curtain wipes)
- 3D chart effects
- Bullet-point walls
- Overly complex infographics that require 30 seconds to decode
- Multiple pie charts side-by-side
- Gradient text (readability nightmare)

---

### 7.3 Format Trends

**Vertical / Mobile-first slides**
- Portrait orientation for executive mobile review
- Scroll-friendly, single-column layout
- Increasingly common for PDF leave-behinds

**Animated / interactive decks**
- Branching navigation for choose-your-own-path content
- Clickable sections, zoom effects
- Tools: Pitch, Beautiful.ai, Tome, Gamma

**Cinemagraph backgrounds**
- Subtle looping motion (rippling water, floating particles)
- Creates mood without distraction
- Use only when subtle; aggressive motion = distraction

## Section 8: Industry Standards

### 8.1 McKinsey Consulting Style

**The Five Commandments:**

1. **Action titles** — Every slide title is a complete declarative sentence stating the insight, not a label. "Revenue declined 8% due to premium segment contraction" beats "Revenue Analysis."

2. **One message per slide** — Each slide advances exactly one argument. When you want to make two points, use two slides.

3. **Pyramid Principle structure** — Recommendation first (Slide 1), then 3–5 supporting points, then evidence. Executives read only titles and still understand the story.

4. **MECE arguments** — Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive. No overlap between supporting points; no important dimensions missing.

5. **Ruthless clarity** — Every visual element must actively serve the message. Anything that doesn't is removed.

**McKinsey Deck Structure:**

| Section | Slides | Purpose |
|---------|--------|---------|
| Cover | 1 | Title, client, date, presenter |
| Executive Summary | 1–2 | Main recommendation + 3–5 supporting points |
| Situation | 2–4 | Current state and context |
| Complication | 2–4 | Problem/opportunity, quantified impact |
| Analysis / Resolution | 10–20 | Supporting arguments with evidence |
| Recommendations | 2–4 | Specific, actionable next steps |
| Appendix | Optional | Supporting analysis for Q&A |

**McKinsey Typography:**
- Sans-serif only: Arial, Helvetica, or Calibri
- Body: 16–18pt; Headlines: 24–28pt; Chart labels: 12–14pt
- Left-align all body content
- Bold for emphasis only; avoid italics and underline
- Single font family throughout

**McKinsey Color:**
- 2–3 primary colors + grey shades
- Dark blue or black for primary text
- Grey for supporting text
- One accent (teal or orange) for key insights
- Red reserved exclusively for problems/warnings

**Ghost Deck Method:** Build the narrative in blank slides first. Write action titles only. Verify the story works before adding any content or design.

---

### 8.2 Apple / Steve Jobs Keynote Style

**Core Rules:**
- One headline, one visual per slide — always
- **3-second rule:** Slides must be understood in 3 seconds or less
- No bullet points — ever
- Text appears as spoken narration support, not as readable notes

> "Today, Apple is going to reinvent the phone." — Steve Jobs (framing promise over specs)

**Apple Design Language:**
- San Francisco typeface (custom) — use Inter or SF Pro as public alternative
- Gradients, device mockups, generous whitespace
- Minimal text overlaid on photography
- Dark backgrounds with white type for drama; white backgrounds for clarity

**The Envelope Technique:** Tease the concept (problem) before the reveal (solution). Build tension before delivering the answer. The MacBook Air "fits in an envelope" reveal is the template.

**Sell outcomes, not features:**
> "The most personal device we've ever created." (outcome framing, not spec listing)

**Rehearsed precision:** Every demo, every transition, every animation is choreographed. Apparent spontaneity is the result of extreme preparation.

### 8.3 TED / TEDx Guidelines

**Official TED Slide Rules:**
- One claim or idea per slide maximum
- Font size: **42pt minimum** (TEDx speaker guide specification)
- Use broadcast-safe zones — keep all important content within the inner 80% of the slide
- Only use images you own or have permission to use; cite Creative Commons sources

**TED's 6 Design Principles** (from presentation expert):

1. **Simple and succinct** — one idea, control what the audience sees
2. **Colors and fonts chosen purposefully** — 1–2 fonts, palette matching tone
3. **Avoid visual clichés** — no lightbulbs, targets, or handshakes; use Unsplash
4. **Streamlined charts** — highlight only the key data, annotate in color
5. **Embrace blank slides** — strategic blank slides focus all attention on the speaker (never use white blanks — use black)
6. **Rehearse slide synchronization** — visuals must match spoken words

**On slide count:** There is no "right" number. Every single slide shown — even blank ones — must be connected with the story being told.

**Minimum font for audience readability from back of room:** 30pt (TED general guidance), 42pt (TEDx specific guide).

### 8.4 Y Combinator / Startup Pitch Decks

**YC's Three-Word Design Standard:**

1. **Legible** — Large, bold text in a clear font with high contrast against background
2. **Simple** — One clear idea per slide; no extra details
3. **Obvious** — The main point is understood at a glance without explanation

**YC Deck Specifications:**
- Optimal slide count: **10–15 slides** (Demo Day: 5–7)
- Text per slide: **30–40 words maximum**
- Font: Sans-serif, **24px (18pt) minimum**
- Design: **One idea per slide, large fonts, white backgrounds**
- Color: High-contrast schemes mandatory

**Empirical data:**
- Investors spend average **10 seconds per slide** during first review
- Total deck review time: **3 minutes 44 seconds** average
- 93% of reviewed decks had design flaws hindering founders
- Only 1% of pitch decks secure investment — quality matters

**YC/Sequoia Pitch Deck Structure:**

| Slide | Content |
|-------|---------|
| 1 | Company purpose / one-sentence pitch |
| 2 | Problem |
| 3 | Solution |
| 4 | Why now? |
| 5 | Market size (TAM/SAM/SOM) |
| 6 | Product (demo or screenshots) |
| 7 | Business model |
| 8 | Traction / metrics |
| 9 | Team |
| 10 | Ask (funding amount + use of funds) |

**Guy Kawasaki's 10/20/30 Rule:**
- **10 slides** maximum
- **20 minutes** maximum
- **30pt font** minimum

## Section 9: Master Checklist

### Phase 1: Before You Open Slide Software

- [ ] Defined the ONE core message of this presentation
- [ ] Identified the audience and what they care about
- [ ] Chosen the narrative structure (Pyramid, Hero's Journey, Sparkline, SCR)
- [ ] Established the design decisions: 2–3 colors, 1–2 fonts, imagery approach
- [ ] Determined slide format (16:9 widescreen) and presentation context (live/leave-behind)
- [ ] Set up grid on master slide (recommend 12-column)

### Phase 2: Structure & Content

- [ ] One idea per slide — no exceptions
- [ ] Each slide title is a declarative sentence (action title), not a label
- [ ] Reading only the slide titles in sequence tells a coherent story
- [ ] Recommendation / key message appears in the first 3 slides
- [ ] Total slide count appropriate for context (10 for pitch, 1/min for presentation)
- [ ] Appendix prepared for anticipated questions

### Phase 3: Typography

- [ ] Maximum 2 fonts (or 1 font with multiple weights)
- [ ] Font sizes: 60pt+ for headlines, 32–40pt for body (live presentations)
- [ ] No text smaller than 24pt on any projected slide
- [ ] No more than 3 font sizes per slide
- [ ] No underlines (implies hyperlink)
- [ ] Font is legible from the back of the room

### Phase 4: Color

- [ ] 3–4 colors maximum across the deck
- [ ] 60-30-10 distribution respected (dominant / secondary / accent)
- [ ] All text meets 4.5:1 contrast ratio against background (WCAG AA)
- [ ] Large text (18pt+) meets 3:1 contrast ratio minimum
- [ ] Design is readable in greyscale
- [ ] Red-green combinations avoided for data encoding
- [ ] Color used to encode consistent meaning (green = positive, red = negative)

### Phase 5: Layout

- [ ] All elements align to grid
- [ ] Generous whitespace — slides don't feel packed
- [ ] Key elements at rule-of-thirds intersections (not center of every slide)
- [ ] Consistent margins across all slides
- [ ] Images are high-resolution (1920×1080+ for full-bleed)
- [ ] Images maintain aspect ratio (not stretched)
- [ ] No visual elements that don't serve the message

### Phase 6: Data Visualization

- [ ] Bar/column charts start at zero
- [ ] Line charts: maximum 6 lines
- [ ] Pie/donut charts: maximum 5–6 slices
- [ ] No 3D chart effects anywhere
- [ ] Chart title is a declarative statement (not a label)
- [ ] Key data point highlighted with accent color
- [ ] Source citation on every chart
- [ ] Legend eliminated or minimized (label directly)

### Phase 7: Storytelling

- [ ] Audience is positioned as the hero, not the presenter
- [ ] At least one STAR moment planned (memorable dramatization, shocking stat, evocative visual)
- [ ] Clear call to action in the final slide
- [ ] Emotional and rational beats alternated (Duarte Sparkline)
- [ ] No slide requires more than 3 seconds to understand

### Phase 8: Technical & Final

- [ ] Animations used sparingly; all transitions are Fade or Dissolve
- [ ] Animation timing: 0.3–0.5 seconds maximum
- [ ] No sound effects
- [ ] Tested on actual projector / output device
- [ ] Colors verified on projection (projectors wash out saturation)
- [ ] Backup PDF version prepared
- [ ] Rehearsed at least once with slides
- [ ] Font embedding confirmed for cross-device compatibility
